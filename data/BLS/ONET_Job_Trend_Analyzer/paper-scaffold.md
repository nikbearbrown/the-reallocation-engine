## ABSTRACT

Public and institutional discourse about AI's impact on employment has
systematically misidentified which work is at risk. Using 13 years of
Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics
data (2012–2024) cross-referenced against O*NET 30.2 cognitive ability
and skill profiles for approximately 1,000 occupations, we show that
AI-driven employment disruption correlates with the cognitive tier
composition of an occupation rather than its industry, technology
adjacency, or occupation label. Occupations requiring high pattern
recognition and specification implementation (Tier 1) show sharp
employment declines post-2022. Occupations requiring social and
relational intelligence (Tier 3) show stable or growing employment.
Occupations requiring metacognitive judgment and causal reasoning
(Tier 4/5) show employment growth with an emerging wage premium.
The occupations the public fears AI will eliminate are largely not
the occupations AI is eliminating. This misidentification has direct
implications for curriculum design, workforce development policy,
and individual career planning.

## 1. INTRODUCTION

When ChatGPT launched in November 2022, the public conversation about
AI and employment accelerated rapidly. Headlines predicted displacement
across white-collar work. The question "will AI take my job?" became
the most searched labor market query of 2023. Surveys showed elevated
anxiety across professional occupations — lawyers, teachers, analysts,
salespeople — many of whom had never previously considered their work
automatable.

The anxiety was real. The targeting was wrong.

This paper presents evidence that AI-driven employment disruption is
already visible in the labor market data — but concentrated in a
specific category of cognitive work that public discourse has largely
overlooked. Meanwhile, the occupations generating the most public
anxiety about replacement appear, in the data, to be among the most
stable.

The distinction that matters is not occupation label, industry, or
technology adjacency. It is the cognitive composition of the work —
specifically, whether the dominant cognitive demand of an occupation
is pattern recognition and specification implementation (work where
AI is now superhuman), or social judgment and causal reasoning (work
where AI is weak to absent).

We organize this distinction using a seven-tier taxonomy of human
cognitive capacities ordered by current AI capability [CITE: series
reference]. This paper focuses on three tiers where the employment
evidence is sharpest:

**Tier 1 (Pattern & Association):** Linguistic pattern recognition,
fact retrieval, syntactic correctness, associative lookup, and
specification implementation. AI capability: superhuman. This is the
cognitive content of boilerplate coding, routine legal document
preparation, and data entry — work that looks like knowledge work
from the outside but is, cognitively, pattern execution.

**Tier 3 (Social & Personal):** Interpersonal intelligence, emotional
attunement, social navigation, persuasion, and relational trust.
AI capability: simulation without feeling. This is the cognitive
content of sales, nursing, teaching, and social work — work that
requires a human to be present because the human connection *is*
the product. Salespeople exist because product pages on websites
do not close deals. The relevant displacer for Tier 3 work was
always the website, not the AI — and the website did not displace them.

**Tier 4/5 (Metacognitive & Causal):** Problem formulation, plausibility
auditing, interpretive judgment, causal model construction, and
counterfactual reasoning. AI capability: poor to absent. This is the
cognitive content of software architecture, management consulting,
health services leadership, and engineering management — work that
requires deciding what problem is worth solving before any tool
can solve it.

Our central empirical claim: the BLS employment data, read against
O*NET cognitive ability profiles, shows these three tiers sorting
in exactly the predicted directions over the 2018–2024 period,
with the divergence accelerating after November 2022.

## 2. DATA AND METHODS

### 2.1 Employment Data

We use Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Employment and Wage
Statistics (OEWS) annual national employment estimates for 2012–2024,
covering approximately 830 detailed occupations per year. Employment
figures are indexed to 2018 (2018 = 100) to enable cross-occupation
comparison on a single scale. The index converts raw employment counts
to a common baseline, making visible the *direction and rate* of change
rather than absolute workforce size.

We select 12 anchor occupations across three tiers — four per tier —
chosen to represent the cognitive profile of each tier, not to
maximize the visual contrast between conditions. [DATA: confirm
selection rationale holds after seeing actual numbers.]

### 2.2 Cognitive Ability and Skill Data

We use O*NET 30.2 (February 2026 release), maintained by the US
Department of Labor. O*NET provides standardized ratings on 52
abilities and 35 skills for approximately 1,000 occupations,
collected from occupation analysts and incumbent workers. Ratings
use a 0–7 Level scale and a separate Importance scale. We use
Level scores throughout, as Level measures how much of each
capacity the work requires — directly relevant to the question
of cognitive substitutability.

We focus on five cognitive abilities for the Tier 1/Tier 4/5 contrast:
Originality, Problem Sensitivity, Deductive Reasoning, Inductive
Reasoning, and Selective Attention. We focus on five skills for
the Tier 3 analysis: Social Perceptiveness, Active Listening,
Persuasion, Instructing, and Service Orientation.

The selection of these dimensions is derived from the tier definitions
independently of the employment outcome. The tier taxonomy was
constructed from first principles about AI capability boundaries,
not from observation of employment trends. [IMPORTANT: this ordering
must be stated clearly to preempt HARKing concern from reviewers.]

### 2.3 The O*NET Job Trend Analyzer

Both datasets are integrated in the O*NET Job Trend Analyzer
(irreduciblyhuman.xyz/onet), a publicly accessible tool built for
the Irreducibly Human curriculum at Bear Brown & Company. The tool
allows any researcher to replicate our occupation selections,
test alternative tier mappings, and extend the analysis to any
of the ~1,000 occupations in the O*NET database. All source data
is public domain. [CITE: O*NET Creative Commons 4.0; BLS OEWS
no copyright restriction.]

## 4. DISCUSSION

### 4.1 The Misidentification

The public conversation about AI and employment has concentrated
anxiety on occupations that are, cognitively, among the most
AI-resistant: teachers, nurses, salespeople, therapists. These
are occupations whose core cognitive demand is Tier 3 — social
presence, relational trust, emotional attunement. The relevant
displacer for this work is not AI. It is the website, the phone
tree, the FAQ. And none of those displaced them.

Meanwhile, the occupations that have already experienced sharp
AI-driven employment decline — programmers, data entry operators,
routine legal support staff — generated less public alarm. These
occupations share a cognitive profile: high Tier 1 demand, moderate
to low Tier 4/5 demand. They look like knowledge work from the
outside. They involve computers. They require education. But their
dominant cognitive demand — pattern recognition, specification
implementation, syntactic correctness — is precisely where current
AI is superhuman.

The misidentification is consequential. Curriculum decisions, hiring
practices, and career planning guided by occupation labels rather
than cognitive profiles will systematically prepare people for the
wrong kind of resilience.

### 4.2 The Senior Developer as Evidence

The Programmer/Developer split is the cleanest available natural
experiment. Both occupations are classified as computer and
mathematical occupations. Both involve writing software. Both
require technical education. Yet their employment trajectories
have diverged sharply since 2018, with the divergence accelerating
after 2022.

The cognitive explanation is straightforward: the senior software
developer is not primarily a coder. They are, in practice, a
problem formulator, a systems architect, a judgment-exerciser who
happens to produce code as an artifact of that judgment. The
boilerplate — the specification-implementation layer — is an
increasingly small fraction of the value they provide. AI code
generation tools commoditized that fraction. They did not touch
the judgment layer.

This is the forklift argument made empirically visible. The
intelligent response to a forklift is not to practice lifting
heavier objects. The senior developer who adapted to AI tools
did not practice writing faster boilerplate. They exercised more
judgment — which is what they were paid for all along.

### 4.3 What the Wage Premium Confirms

The 56% wage premium for AI-fluent workers documented by PwC
(2025 Global AI Jobs Barometer, ~1 billion job postings analyzed)
is consistent with the pattern we observe in employment data.
Crucially, the PwC analysis shows the premium applies across
industries — not just in technology. AI-fluent marketing managers,
financial analysts, and operations specialists all command the
premium over their non-AI-fluent peers in the same role.

This is not a "become a data scientist" premium. It is a premium
for domain expertise combined with AI fluency — Tier 4/5 judgment
applied through Tier 1 tools. The wage market is pricing exactly
the combination the taxonomy predicts should be most valuable.

### 4.4 Limitations

**Occupation-level analysis obscures within-occupation variation.**
The BLS codes capture job titles, not cognitive task distributions.
A "Software Developer" at a startup doing greenfield architecture
and a "Software Developer" at a large enterprise maintaining legacy
systems have different cognitive profiles despite the same SOC code.
As AI automates the maintenance layer, the employment index for
Software Developers may eventually bifurcate in ways our current
data cannot see.

**The 12-occupation sample is illustrative, not exhaustive.**
The O*NET Job Trend Analyzer (irreduciblyhuman.xyz/onet) allows
any researcher to extend this analysis to any of the ~1,000
occupations in the database and test alternative tier mappings.
We make no claim that our 12 anchor occupations are a
representative sample — they are theory-driven selections
designed to test whether the pattern is present, not to
estimate its magnitude across the full labor market.

**Causation is not established.** The employment index shows
correlation between cognitive tier profile and employment trajectory.
We attribute the post-2022 acceleration to AI capability growth
because the timing is consistent and no competing explanation
accounts for the abruptness of the change. But this paper does
not establish a causal mechanism. That is the work of a follow-on
study with task-level data.

## 5. CONCLUSION

The AI era is not eliminating work. It is sorting it — by the
cognitive composition of what the work actually requires humans
to do.

The data shows this sorting is already happening. Tier 1 work —
pattern execution, specification implementation, syntactic
correctness — is being automated. The occupations whose dominant
demand is Tier 1 are declining. The occupations whose dominant
demand is Tier 3 (relational presence) or Tier 4/5 (judgment and
causal reasoning) are holding or growing.

The misidentification of AI risk — widespread anxiety about
AI-resistant occupations, relative calm about AI-vulnerable ones —
is not a minor error in public discourse. It is an educational
emergency. Curricula that optimize for Tier 1 capacities are
preparing students for the fraction of work that is most exposed.
Curricula that develop Tier 3, 4, and 5 capacities are preparing
students for the work that remains irreducibly human.

The sorting is visible in the data. The curriculum has not yet
caught up.
